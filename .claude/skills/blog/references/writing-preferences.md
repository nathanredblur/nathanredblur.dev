# Writing Preferences & Style Guide

This file defines how Nathan's blog posts should sound, look, and feel.
Update this file when preferences evolve, new patterns emerge, or feedback reveals what works.

---

## Voice & Tone

- **First person always**: "I", "my", "I've found that...", "In my experience..."
- **Conversational but knowledgeable**: like explaining to a smart friend
- **Direct and actionable**: get to the point quickly
- **Confident but humble**: share expertise without being condescending
- **Honest opinions**: Nathan has preferences and shares them — including downsides
- **Admits uncertainty**: says when something is new or experimental
- **Natural humor**: when it fits, never forced

## Banned Language

These are automatic red flags. Never use them — they make content feel generic, AI-generated, or dishonest.

### Marketing & Corporate Speak

- "We're excited/thrilled to announce" — just announce it
- "Best-in-class" / "industry-leading" / "cutting-edge" — show, don't tell
- "Seamless" / "seamlessly" — nothing is seamless
- "Empower" / "leverage" / "unlock" — say what you actually mean
- "Robust" — describe what makes it robust instead
- "Streamline" — everyone is streamlining, stop
- "Synergy", "paradigm shift" — no
- "At [Company], we believe..." — just state the belief

### AI Tell-Tale Phrases

- "Let's dive in" / "Without further ado" — just start
- "In this blog post, we will explore..." — be direct, just start
- "In today's fast-paced world of technology..." — filler, cut it
- "As you might know" / "As we all know..." — if we all know, why are you writing it?

### Dismissive Phrases

- "Simply do X" — nothing is simple if you're reading a tutorial
- "It's easy to..." — dismissive of the reader's experience
- "Obviously..." — if it's obvious, don't write it
- "Just do X" — minimizes the reader's effort

### Filler & Hedging

- "That being said," / "It's worth noting that," / "At the end of the day," — filler transitions, cut them
- "In conclusion..." — just conclude naturally
- "It could be argued that..." — just argue it
- "game-changer", "chaos", "disaster", "revolutionary", "groundbreaking" — hype without substance
- "absolutely", "literally" (unless literal), "incredibly", "insanely" — filler superlatives
- "This is amazing!" — instead, explain *what* you like and *why*

## Expressions & Patterns that WORK

Based on Nathan's actual writing voice:

- **Direct openers**: "I use StyleX every day at work. And I hate it."
- **Personal context first**: "A friend and I split the cost of an expensive course..."
- **Conversational transitions**: "Here's where it gets interesting.", "Sounds great, right? Here's where it falls apart."
- **Honest framing**: "Don't get me wrong — [thing] solves real problems at [context]. But..."
- **Specific over vague**: name the tools, versions, platforms — not "a popular tool"
- **Questions to the reader**: rhetorical questions that set up the next section

## Framing Strategy

Choose the opening approach based on article type:

- **Credibility-first**: Start with external authority ("Every developer needs...") when building trust with unfamiliar readers
- **Personal-first**: Start with your perspective ("I wanted...", "I used to hate...") when sharing opinions, experiences, or making the blog feel intimate

Both are valid. Pick based on what the article needs.

## Formatting Rules

- **Paragraphs**: 2-4 lines maximum
- **Sentences**: max 20 words average
- **Headers**: should tell a story when scanned alone
- **Lists**: for 3+ items of clear, concise points; less necessary in narrative articles
- **Code blocks**: always with syntax highlighting and language tag
- **Bold**: key concepts and important terms
- **Links**: descriptive anchor text (never "click here")
- **Conciseness**: don't pad for word count — if fewer words work, use them
- **Invite rather than instruct**: questions engage better than commands, especially at the end

## Language

- **Always English**
- **American English** spelling (color, not colour)
- **Technical terms**: use widely accepted terminology
- **Jargon**: explain it for beginner content, assume it for advanced

## Content Quality Signals

What makes a Nathan blog post feel authentic:

- Personal anecdotes that set up the topic (the Hotmart course story, the Pinterest inspiration board)
- Honest critique of tools he actually uses (StyleX rant, Tailwind evolution)
- Specific commands and configs readers can copy-paste
- Admonitions (tip, note, warning) for important callouts
- GitHub repo cards for tools mentioned (::github{repo="owner/repo"})
- Real screenshots when they add value
